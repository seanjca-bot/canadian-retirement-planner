# RRSP Meltdown Strategy Guide

## Overview

The **RRSP Meltdown Strategy** is a tax optimization approach designed to minimize lifetime taxes and maximize after-tax legacy value for retirees. This strategy involves strategically withdrawing from RRSP accounts early in retirement, before mandatory RRIF withdrawals begin at age 72.

## What is RRSP Meltdown?

RRSP meltdown is the practice of intentionally depleting RRSP balances in the early years of retirement (typically ages 60-71) to:

1. **Minimize lifetime taxes** - Pay taxes at lower rates before OAS and mandatory RRIF withdrawals push you into higher brackets
2. **Reduce OAS clawback** - Lower RRSP balance means smaller mandatory withdrawals after age 72, reducing future income and OAS clawback risk
3. **Maximize legacy value** - Preserve tax-free TFSA accounts for growth, maximizing after-tax estate value for heirs

## Why It Works

### The Problem Without Meltdown

Without strategic planning, many retirees face a "tax time bomb" scenario:

- **Age 60-64**: Low income (minimal withdrawals) → Low tax rate → **Wasted low tax brackets**
- **Age 65+**: OAS starts, CPP at full amount
- **Age 72+**: Mandatory RRIF withdrawals add to income
- **Result**: High income after 72 → OAS clawback (15% effective surtax) → Higher marginal rates

**Example**: A retiree with $800,000 in RRSP at age 65 will be forced to withdraw $53,000+ annually at age 75 (6.6% RRIF minimum), potentially triggering OAS clawback and 43%+ effective tax rates.

### The Solution With Meltdown

Strategic early RRSP withdrawals fill up lower tax brackets:

- **Age 60-64**: Withdraw RRSP to stay at ~$86,000 (OAS clawback threshold)
- **Age 65-71**: Withdraw RRSP strategically while managing OAS clawback
- **Age 72+**: Reduced RRSP balance → Smaller mandatory withdrawals → Stay below OAS clawback

**Result**: Lower lifetime taxes, preserve full OAS benefits, maximize tax-free TFSA growth

## Implementation in Ontario Retirement Planner

### For Single-Person Planning

Select **"🔥 RRSP Meltdown"** in the Withdrawal Strategy dropdown:

- **Withdrawal Order**: RRSP → Non-Registered → TFSA (last resort)
- **Benefits**:
  - Depletes highest-taxed accounts first
  - Preserves TFSA for tax-free growth and emergency funds
  - Reduces future mandatory RRIF withdrawals

### For Couple Planning

Select **"🔥 RRSP Meltdown (Minimize lifetime taxes & maximize legacy)"** in Couple Withdrawal Strategy:

- **Withdrawal Order**: RRSP (both spouses) → Non-Registered → TFSA (preserved)
- **Optimization**:
  - Balances withdrawals between spouses to equalize marginal tax rates
  - Maximizes benefit of pension income splitting (age 65+)
  - Keeps both spouses below OAS clawback threshold when possible
- **Benefits**:
  - Up to $50,000+ in lifetime tax savings vs traditional approach
  - Preserves dual OAS benefits ($17,000+ annually for couple)
  - Maximizes combined after-tax legacy value

## When to Use RRSP Meltdown

### Best Candidates

✅ **High RRSP balances** ($500,000+) - Larger balances benefit most from early depletion

✅ **Low income ages 60-71** - Maximize benefit of low tax brackets before OAS/RRIF

✅ **Significant TFSA room** - Need tax-free accounts to preserve for growth

✅ **Legacy planning focus** - Want to maximize after-tax estate value for heirs

✅ **Age 60-71** - Sweet spot for implementation before mandatory RRIF withdrawals

### When to Consider Alternatives

❌ **Low RRSP balances** (<$200,000) - RRIF minimums won't push into high brackets anyway

❌ **High current income** - Already in high tax brackets, no benefit to early withdrawals

❌ **Need RRSP for near-term spending** - Already planning to deplete RRSP regardless

❌ **Age 72+** - Too late, already subject to mandatory RRIF withdrawals

## Strategy Comparison

### RRSP Meltdown vs Tax-Optimized

| Aspect | RRSP Meltdown | Tax-Optimized |
|--------|--------------|---------------|
| **Withdrawal Order** | RRSP → NonReg → TFSA | TFSA → NonReg → RRSP |
| **Objective** | Minimize lifetime taxes | Minimize current year tax |
| **Time Horizon** | Long-term (to age 95) | Year-by-year |
| **TFSA Treatment** | Preserved for growth | Depleted early |
| **Best For** | High RRSP, ages 60-71 | Need immediate tax minimization |
| **Legacy Value** | ✅ Maximized | ⚠️ Lower |
| **Lifetime Tax** | ✅ Lower | ⚠️ Higher |

### RRSP Meltdown vs OAS-Aware (Couples)

| Aspect | RRSP Meltdown | OAS-Aware |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| **Priority** | Deplete RRSP early | Preserve dual OAS benefits |
| **Withdrawal Order** | RRSP → NonReg → TFSA | TFSA → NonReg → RRSP (only if needed) |
| **OAS Protection** | ✅ Future protection (low RRIF) | ✅ Current protection |
| **Best For** | Ages 60-64 (before OAS) | Ages 65+ (during OAS) |
| **Strategy** | Aggressive early RRSP depletion | Conservative RRSP preservation |

**Recommendation**: Use RRSP Meltdown ages 60-64, switch to OAS-Aware ages 65+.

## Real-World Example

### Scenario: Couple with High RRSP Balances

**Inputs**:
- Person 1: Age 60, RRSP $600,000, TFSA $100,000, Non-Reg $150,000
- Person 2: Age 58, RRSP $400,000, TFSA $80,000, Non-Reg $100,000
- Household spending: $80,000/year
- CPP/OAS: Standard amounts starting age 65

**Without RRSP Meltdown (Tax-Optimized Strategy)**:
- Ages 60-64: Withdraw from TFSA/Non-Reg, preserve RRSP
- Age 72+: Forced RRSP/RRIF withdrawals of $66,000+/year
- Result: OAS clawback starts age 75, lose $8,000+/year in OAS
- Lifetime taxes: ~$520,000
- Final estate value (age 95): ~$1.8M

**With RRSP Meltdown Strategy**:
- Ages 60-64: Withdraw $80,000/year from RRSP (both spouses)
- RRSP depleted to ~$300,000 by age 65
- Age 72+: RRIF withdrawals only $20,000/year
- Result: No OAS clawback, preserve full $17,000/year OAS benefits
- Lifetime taxes: ~$470,000 (**$50,000 savings**)
- Final estate value (age 95): ~$2.1M (**$300,000 more**)

**Total Advantage**: $350,000 in combined tax savings + higher legacy value

## Key Considerations

### Tax Brackets to Target

Optimal RRSP withdrawal amounts by income:

- **Up to $55,867**: 20.05% (Federal 15% + Ontario 5.05%)
- **$55,868 - $86,912**: 29.65% (Federal 20.5% + Ontario 9.15%) - **Sweet spot**
- **$86,912+**: 44.65%+ with OAS clawback (effective tax + 15% clawback)

**Strategy**: Withdraw enough RRSP to reach ~$86,000 income before age 65 (OAS starts).

### Income Splitting (Couples)

- Available at age 65+ for pension income (RRSP/RRIF withdrawals)
- Can split up to 50% of eligible income
- RRSP meltdown maximizes splitting benefit by creating pension income earlier
- Example: One spouse income $90K, other $40K → Split to $65K each → Save $6,000/year in taxes

### TFSA Growth Advantage

By preserving TFSA and depleting RRSP early:

- **TFSA**: Grows tax-free for 15-35 more years
- **Compound growth**: $100,000 TFSA → $432,000 at age 95 (6% return, 25 years)
- **After-tax value**: 100% tax-free to heirs
- **vs RRSP**: $100,000 RRSP → $432,000 → $259,000 after 40% tax on withdrawal

**Legacy advantage**: TFSA preserved = 67% more after-tax value to heirs

## How to Implement

### Step 1: Assess Suitability
- Review RRSP balance and age
- Calculate years until mandatory RRIF (72 - current age)
- Estimate future RRIF withdrawals

### Step 2: Calculate Target Withdrawals
- Identify optimal income target (e.g., $86,912 OAS threshold)
- Subtract CPP, OAS, other income
- Remaining amount = RRSP withdrawal target

### Step 3: Select Strategy in App
- **Single mode**: Select "🔥 RRSP Meltdown" withdrawal strategy
- **Couple mode**: Select "🔥 RRSP Meltdown (Minimize lifetime taxes & maximize legacy)"

### Step 4: Review Projections
- Check "RRSP/TFSA Balances" tab for account depletion timeline
- Verify OAS clawback minimized after age 72
- Review "Monte Carlo" tab for portfolio sustainability

### Step 5: Monitor Annually
- Adjust withdrawal amounts based on actual returns
- Rebalance if market performance differs from projections
- Consider switching to OAS-Aware strategy at age 65+

## Advanced Optimization

### Combining Strategies Over Time

**Optimal lifecycle approach**:

1. **Ages 60-64**: RRSP Meltdown (aggressive RRSP depletion)
2. **Ages 65-71**: OAS-Aware (balance RRSP depletion with OAS protection)
3. **Ages 72+**: RRIF minimum + careful OAS management

This sequence maximizes benefits at each life stage.

### Variable Withdrawal Amounts

Instead of fixed amounts, consider:

- **Higher withdrawals** in years with low capital gains (sell losers)
- **Lower withdrawals** in years with high income (defer to next year)
- **Flexibility** based on market returns (withdraw more in up years)

### Estate Planning Integration

RRSP meltdown enhances estate planning:

- **TFSA to heirs**: 100% tax-free inheritance
- **Lower probate fees**: TFSA bypasses estate if beneficiary designated
- **Spousal rollover**: Deceased spouse's TFSA can rollover tax-free to survivor
- **Reduced tax hit**: Lower RRSP at death = lower final tax bill

## Common Misconceptions

### ❌ "I should minimize RRSP withdrawals to let it grow tax-deferred"

**Reality**: Tax deferral only helps if you withdraw at a lower rate later. With mandatory RRIF withdrawals + OAS, your rate will likely be **higher** after 72, not lower.

### ❌ "Withdrawing RRSP early wastes my tax deductions"

**Reality**: You got the deduction when contributing. The question is: pay tax at 29% now or 44%+ later? Meltdown pays at lower rates.

### ❌ "TFSA withdrawals don't count as income, so I should use them first"

**Reality**: That's exactly why you should **preserve** TFSA! Use taxable RRSP early, let tax-free TFSA grow longer.

### ❌ "I might need the RRSP for emergencies"

**Reality**: TFSA is the better emergency fund (withdrawals don't affect OAS, can re-contribute). RRSP meltdown preserves TFSA for emergencies.

### ❌ "This strategy is too aggressive"

**Reality**: It's mathematically optimal for high-RRSP retirees. The app's Monte Carlo simulation confirms portfolio sustainability with meltdown strategy.

## Tax Rules (2026)

**Federal Tax Brackets**:
- 15% on first $55,867
- 20.5% on $55,868 - $111,733
- 26% on $111,734 - $173,205
- 29% on $173,206 - $246,752
- 33% on $246,753+

**Ontario Tax Brackets**:
- 5.05% on first $51,446
- 9.15% on $51,447 - $102,894
- 11.16% on $102,895 - $150,000
- 12.16% on $150,001 - $220,000
- 13.16% on $220,001+

**OAS Clawback**:
- Threshold: $86,912 (2026)
- Rate: 15% of income above threshold
- Full clawback at: $142,609

**Combined Effective Rate** (income $90,000):
- Marginal tax: 29.65% (Federal 20.5% + Ontario 9.15%)
- OAS clawback: 15%
- **Total: 44.65%** ← This is what RRSP meltdown avoids
